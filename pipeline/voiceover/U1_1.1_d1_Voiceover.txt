[Slide 3]
Three targets today, all from the first two goals for this topic. First, define social engineering and identify its indicators — the attacker’s goal (get you to reveal information, download a file, or click a link) and the channel it arrives through (in person, email, text, or social media). Second, the two tactics the College Board names by name — intimidation and urgency — and being able to point to each one in a real message. Third, not just naming the tactics but explaining why they work on human beings — intimidation leverages our fear of negative consequences, and urgency pressures us to act before we stop to think. By the end of today you should be able to read a suspicious message and say what tactic it uses and why that tactic is effective.

[Slide 4]

First, what social engineering is: the definition, the channels it uses, and the two tactics the exam names. Second, why those tactics work — the human instincts they exploit. And third, we apply it to a real phishing email in Scenario 1A.

[Slide 5]
Section one. Before we can defend against it, we have to be able to name it. Social engineering is the oldest trick in cybersecurity, and the College Board is very specific about what counts and which tactics to watch for. Let's lock down the words.

[Slide 6]
Four words. Social engineering is using psychological tactics to manipulate people, not technology, into one of three things: revealing sensitive information, downloading a malicious file, or clicking a malicious link. That word for getting someone to reveal information is elicitation. Then the two tactics the standard names for this topic: intimidation is threatening a target with negative consequences if they don't comply — think the account-freeze threat from the bell ringer. And urgency is creating reasons the target should act quickly — the ten-minute deadline. Just two named tactics in 1.1; we'll meet more of them later in the course, but for this topic, intimidation and urgency are the whole list.

[Slide 7]
Where does this reach you? In person sometimes, but far more often by email, text message, or social media message — the channels you use every day. And the attacker wants one of exactly three outcomes: you reveal sensitive information, you download a malicious file, or you click a malicious link. Notice there's no hacking in the Hollywood sense here — the weakness being exploited is human behavior. And watch for stacking: a real message will often combine tactics, like a threat plus a deadline, to pile on the pressure.

[Slide 8]
Section two. It's not enough to name intimidation and urgency — the exam wants you to explain WHY they work. And the answer is the same for both: they rely on common psychological principles that influence everyone's behavior. They target instinct, not intelligence.

[Slide 9]
Two tactics, two instincts. Intimidation leverages a natural human aversion to negative consequences: by drawing attention to a bad outcome, the adversary uses fear to push you to act — and the threat doesn't even have to be real. Urgency leverages our natural reflex to react quickly when something is time-sensitive: a deadline pressures you to respond fast, and that speed is the point, because it stops you from pausing to ask whether the request is even reasonable or safe. On the exam, naming the tactic is half the answer; explaining the instinct it exploits is the other half.

[Slide 10]
Here's the trap: “I'd never fall for that.” We assume awareness equals protection. But intimidation and urgency target instinct, not intelligence — your fear of a bad outcome, your reflex to beat a deadline. They work on trained professionals all the time. The bank text fooled careful people precisely because the ten-minute clock never gave them a chance to call the real bank and check. The defense isn't being smart or suspicious; it's having a habit: slow down and verify through a second, trusted channel before you act.

[Slide 11]
Let's put it to work. This is College Board Scenario 1A, Detecting Phishing Messages. The setup: you're sitting next to your teacher at their desk when an email arrives. Your teacher is about to click the link, but something feels off to you. Your job is to decide whether it's legitimate and to be ready to back that up with evidence. Here's the email.

[Slide 12]
Read it the way your teacher did — fast, in the middle of something else. Now slow down. Look at the sender: do-not-reply at g-zero-zero-gle dot com. Those are zeros, not the letter o — that's a look-alike domain, not Google. The subject is bracket-Urgent-bracket: that's urgency, manufactured right in the subject line. The body warns that the student won't be able to finish their assignment — a negative consequence, that's intimidation, leaning on the teacher's sense of responsibility. And that last line about “research shows faster responses” is pure pressure to act without thinking. No real evidence, just a push.
