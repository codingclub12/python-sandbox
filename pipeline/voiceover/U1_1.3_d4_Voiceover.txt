[Slide 6]
Q1. Correct answer is A, jamming. The question signals the attack plainly — high-power transmitter, frequency band flooding, preventing legitimate access. That is the textbook definition of jamming as a denial-of-service attack. The most common miss was C, war driving, because students see 'parking garage' and pattern-match to war driving. The deciding factor is always: is the attacker TRANSMITTING to disrupt, or RECEIVING to map? Transmitting to flood is jamming every time.

[Slide 7]
Q2. Correct answer is C. C has two separate errors: the anonymity claim is false and the end-to-end encryption claim is false — a VPN encrypts to the VPN operator only, not to the final destination. If you got this wrong, almost certainly it's because you missed the word INCORRECT. A, B, and D are all accurate statements — circling INCORRECT before you read choices would have locked this answer in immediately.

[Slide 8]
Q3. Correct answer is B, I and II only. III is the error — the analyst in the question got it wrong. Modifying an existing jamming tool to target a specific frequency band is exactly what's classified as high-skilled: 'modify existing ones to adapt.' That's not low-skilled because it required modifying the tool, not just deploying it as-is. If you picked D, you trusted the analyst. The question asked you to check the analyst's work.

[Slide 9]
Q4. Correct answer is D. A, B, and C are all genuine core defenses — so if you picked one of those, you missed the word LEAST. D sounds reasonable as a security habit: change your password before using risky Wi-Fi. The problem is timing. The evil twin intercepts whatever you log in with during the session. If you just changed your password, the attacker gets the new one. Password rotation is a post-compromise response, not a pre-session defense. Circle LEAST before reading — this question rewards that habit.

[Slide 10]
Q5. Correct answer is C, war driving. Every detail in the scenario maps to the definition: detecting beacons, gathering network information, finding where the signal extends outside the building. The most common miss was D — students who know war driving is often legal assumed it couldn't be a classified attack. Legality and classification are separate questions. War driving is named as a wireless attack type. That's the answer.

[Slide 11]
Here's a quick bridge. Everything in 1.3 required an attacker to show up somewhere with equipment. That's a real constraint. Lesson 1.4 is about what happens when AI removes that constraint. The attacks scale. The personalization improves. A low-skilled adversary with an AI tool can generate spear-phishing content that previously required a skilled human to research and write. Scenario 1D is a deepfake impersonation scenario — same social engineering principles from 1.1, now running at machine speed and volume.
