[Slide 9]
Sign 1. Look carefully: this log shows successful sign-ins only. There is no failure count anywhere in the eight entries — so the honest answer is that Sign 1, many failed attempts in a short duration, is NOT clearly shown in this log. Say that explicitly; do not invent a failure burst because you expected one. On a real system the attacker may well have failed some attempts before entries 4 and 7, but you can only cite what the log shows. And that is fine — the standard does not require all three signs. One or more is enough to flag suspicious activity, and as you are about to see, Signs 2 and 3 are both clearly present on the same two entries. The exam will sometimes hand you explicit failure counts and sometimes an anomaly pattern like this one. Know both formats, and only claim the signs you can evidence.

[Slide 10]
Sign 2. The scenario tells you exactly when this student normally logs in: weekends and evenings when not in school. Look at entries 4 and 7 — Wednesday 11:13 a.m. And Monday 10:07 a.m. That is school time on school days. The legitimate user is almost certainly in class at those times. Sign 2 is present and clearly evidenced by comparing the attack timestamps against the scenario’s stated user pattern. This is the most reliable sign in the Scenario 1B log.

[Slide 11]
Sign 3. Every legitimate login in this log — entries 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 8 — is from 208.104.29.211. That is the student’s home IP. Entries 4 and 7 are from 142.54.195.17. That IP has never appeared in this account’s history before these two entries. Unknown device slash unknown IP. Sign 3 is present. And notice: signs 2 and 3 both point to the exact same two entries. That is not coincidence. When multiple signs converge on the same log entries, you have near-certain evidence of unauthorized access.

[Slide 12]
Here is the scenario's teaching moment. Entries 4 and 7 are successful logins. The student did not notice because of a burst of failed attempts — they noticed because someone modified their game choices. That is how many real breaches are discovered: not by catching the attack in progress, but by finding the damage afterward. Now apply. The family name Rivera is our added detail — the official scenario names no family, and the log itself only shows entries, days, times, and IPs. An adversary who learned that name, from social media or anywhere else, almost certainly built a dictionary combining it with common year patterns and trailing special characters. That is the exact attack mechanism the standard describes. All three defenses would have helped: a random password defeats the dictionary, avoiding the family name removes the anchor, and MFA blocks the login even after the password is cracked.

[Slide 13]
Summary. Two signs present — unusual times and unknown IP — and both point at the same two entries, 4 and 7. Sign 1 is not shown, and saying so out loud is part of a full-credit answer, because this log records successes only. The attack still succeeded: the adversary most likely matched the password with a dictionary anchored on the family name, which is our added detail — the log shows only entries, days, times, and IPs. And all three defenses would have helped here. This is the AP exam analysis template: signs — with honest evidence — then mechanism, then defenses. You will use this exact structure on Day 3 with new scenarios.

[Slide 14]
Question one: the family name Rivera is our added detail — the log never shows a name. How does an adversary who learned that name turn it into a dictionary? What did the word list likely contain? Be specific — name plus years, name plus trailing special characters. Question two: the account owner wants to prevent this from happening again. Recommend two of the three defenses and explain the mechanism. Not just name them — explain how each one would have stopped this specific attack.

[Slide 15]
Enrichment. Day 2's log raised a fair question: why would a log show only successes? Two server-side controls shape what an attack looks like in the record. Account lockout freezes the account after a threshold of rapid failures — it stops automated tools cold, at the cost of enabling denial-of-service against the user. Rate limiting slows attempts down instead: an attacker forced to space out guesses leaves no dramatic burst, which is one reason a login log can end up looking like Scenario 1B's — quiet, with only the successes recorded. Remember the layer distinction for the exam: lockout and rate limiting are server-side controls; the three defenses are actions the individual user takes. Both layers together, plus MFA, is the full posture.
