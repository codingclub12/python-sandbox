Start with the idea that holds the whole day together: adversaries often use social engineering when conducting a physical attack. Social engineering means manipulating people into doing something that compromises security. The adversary doesn't pick the lock — they get a person to open it. A door is only as strong as the human willing to hold it. That's why almost every attack we name today has a person being tricked at its center: someone holds a door, someone looks away, someone trusts a uniform. When you read an exam scenario, find the manipulated human first — that's your signal that you're looking at a social-engineering-based physical attack, and it helps you pick the right name.

[Slide 7]

[Slide 8]
Three attacks that get an adversary physically into a restricted area. First, piggybacking: the adversary uses social engineering to manipulate an authorized person into granting access. The classic tactics are in the standard verbatim — carrying something large so you hold the door, pretending to have forgotten an access token, or posing as a maintenance worker who needs in for an inspection or repair. Key word: the authorized person knowingly helps. Second, tailgating: the adversary follows close behind an authorized person without that person's awareness or knowledge. Key difference: the authorized person never knowingly grants access — they don't notice at all. Third, card cloning: the adversary copies an authorized user's access card and then gains all the access that user has. The piggybacking-versus-tailgating distinction: knowingly helped versus unknowingly followed.

[Slide 9]
Two attacks that don't require getting through any door — the adversary harvests information that's already exposed. On the left, shoulder surfing: the adversary watches as a user accesses sensitive information so the adversary can use it later. Think watching someone type a PIN or a door code over their shoulder. And the standard adds: sometimes they use a camera to record the target for later analysis. On the right, dumpster diving: the adversary goes through a target's physical trash looking for information that could help them reach their goal — discarded printouts, sticky notes with passwords, old hardware. Both of these steal information without ever entering a restricted space. On the exam, name the source: eyes on a live screen is shoulder surfing; paper in the trash is dumpster diving.

[Slide 10]
Quick enrichment on the two attacks you will most want to confuse. Both get an adversary past a controlled door, but the mechanism is opposite. On the left, piggybacking: the authorized person knowingly grants access because they were manipulated — the heavy box, the forgotten-badge story, the fake maintenance call. They are aware they let someone in; they were tricked into it. On the right, tailgating: the adversary follows close behind without the person's awareness — no interaction, no trick, just timing. The authorized person never knew anyone was there. So one question separates them on the exam: did the authorized person knowingly grant access? Yes, it's piggybacking. No, it's tailgating. Notice the defenses differ too — piggybacking is defeated by training people, tailgating by hardware like turnstiles and mantraps.

Piggybacking: authorized person tricked into granting access. Tailgating: followed without awareness. Shoulder surfing: watched accessing info. Dumpster diving: trash. Card cloning: copied card. And social engineering is the thread tying them together. Know all six by name.
