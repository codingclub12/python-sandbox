Welcome back to Lesson 2.1, Cyber Foundations. The first three days were the attacker's side: tactics, adversaries, and attack phases. Today, we switch to the defender's first job — measuring risk. No organization can fix everything, so they have to decide which dangers matter most. Risk, the College Board says, occurs when a threat can exploit a vulnerability to compromise an asset. The risk assessment process weighs two things — how likely an attack is and how severe the damage would be — and produces a rating that can be quantitative or qualitative. By the end of today you can carry out that process.

[Slide 2]
A school has two gaps. Gap A: a weak lock on a closet full of old furniture. Gap B: the server room with every student's records, protected by a default password that's published online for that exact device. Which do you fix first, and why? Here's the real question — what two things did you weigh to make that call? And would your answer flip if the closet held the records instead of furniture? You just did a risk assessment by instinct; today we'll name the two factors you used — likelihood and severity — and make it formal.

[Slide 3]
Three sections, the first two core, the last enrichment. Section one defines risk and its two factors — likelihood and severity — and the things that drive each. Section two is the output: a rating that's quantitative or qualitative, and the documentation a real risk assessment must include. Section three is a quick enrichment on the College Board's own example — the hacktivist who'd attack a food company's webpage even though there's no money in it — to show that ‘value’ depends on the adversary's motivation.

[Slide 4]
Three things to leave with today, describe the risk assessment process. First, define risk as a threat exploiting a vulnerability to compromise an asset, and assess it with the two factors the College Board names — likelihood and severity. Second, explain what drives likelihood: the value of the target, the skill the exploit requires, and the motivation and capability of likely adversaries. Third, tell a quantitative result — a number or a dollar figure — from a qualitative one — low, medium, high, severe — and list what the documentation must contain. The exam hands you an asset, a vulnerability, and a likely threat, and asks you to reason about its risk; by the end of today you can do exactly that.

[Slide 5]
Section one. First the definition of risk, then the two factors every assessment weighs — likelihood and severity — and what drives each.

[Slide 6]
Two columns of definitions you must own. On the left: risk occurs when a threat can exploit a vulnerability to compromise an asset. Three pieces — threat, vulnerability, asset — and all three must be present for there to be risk. And: an asset is anything valuable — financial resources, intellectual property, data, digital infrastructure, physical property, and reputation. Reputation counts as an asset; don't forget it. On the right: a risk assessment considers exactly two factors — the likelihood of an attack against a specific vulnerability, and the severity of the projected damage. That's the whole framework: likelihood times severity, in effect. Highest likelihood and highest severity is what you fix first — that's your bell-ringer server room.

[Slide 7]
Now the likelihood drivers. The standard says likelihood depends on many factors, and names three. One, the value of the target: adversaries go after what they perceive as valuable, so a high-value asset is more likely to be hit. Two, the skill required, the difficulty: vulnerabilities with well-documented exploits need less skill, so more adversaries can pull them off — that's exactly your bell-ringer's published default password, low difficulty, high likelihood. Three, the motivation and capability of likely adversaries: highly motivated, highly skilled attackers can perform complex exploits, so even a hard vulnerability gets risky when the right adversary is interested. Those are the three likelihood drivers. Hold on to the other factor too — severity, which we just saw is measured by financial cost plus reputational and operational impacts.

[Slide 8]
Quick enrichment on the College Board's own example, because it makes one idea click. A hacktivist cares deeply about illegal fishing that a food company supports. To that hacktivist, the company's main webpage is a high-value target — defacing it earns zero dollars, but it raises awareness of the cause that drives them. So here's the lesson: ‘value of the target’ is judged by the adversary, not by a price tag. Tie it back to Day 2 — a hacktivist values exposure; a transnational criminal organization values money. The very same webpage is high-value to one and worthless to the other. Which means likelihood is adversary-specific — you can't assess it without asking who would attack and what they want.

[Slide 9]
Section two. We've assessed the risk — now we record it. The result is quantitative or qualitative, and the documentation has required contents.

[Slide 10]
Two columns for the output. On the left: the result of a risk assessment is quantitative or qualitative. Quantitative assigns a number — a 1-to-10 score, or a dollar figure like a $10,000 annual risk. Qualitative uses words — low, medium, high, severe, or combinations like ‘likely high impact.’ Both are valid; know which is which. On the right, the documentation, which the exam can test as a list: the vulnerable assets and their value; descriptions of likely threats and the specific vulnerabilities and how they'd be exploited; the severity of the damage — financial, operational, reputational — and the likelihood of compromise; and a final rating, quantitative or qualitative, for each risk. Notice it folds in everything from today: assets, threats, vulnerabilities, severity, likelihood, rating.

[Slide 11]
Six terms. The risk equation is first: risk is a threat exploiting a vulnerability to compromise an asset, and an asset is anything valuable — including reputation. Then the two factors: likelihood, the probability against a specific vulnerability, and severity, the size of the damage. Finally the two output types: quantitative is numbers — a 1-to-10 score or a dollar figure — and qualitative is words — low, medium, high, severe — both. Be able to tell quantitative from qualitative on sight.
