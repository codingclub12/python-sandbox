[Slide 1]
Welcome to Cyber Foundations. This is a big topic: eight class periods that build the vocabulary the rest of the course rests on. Today, Day 1, covers social engineering attacks. Before an adversary writes a single line of malicious code, the easiest way into a system is often a person. Social engineers use psychological tactics — pretexting, authority, intimidation, consensus, scarcity, urgency, and familiarity — to manipulate a target into doing what the attacker wants. Over the next eight days we'll go from these human attacks all the way to defense-in-depth. Today we start with the con before the code.

[Slide 2]
Read it carefully: someone calls claiming to be IT, says your account was flagged in an audit, and demands your password in ten minutes or you lose access and your manager hears about it — and he already knows your name and department. In three to five sentences, list every trick in that call. What does he get by sounding official? By knowing your details? By putting you on a clock? Every single pressure tactic in that call has a name in today's standard, and by the end you'll be able to label each one.

[Slide 3]
Three sections today, the first two core, the last one enrichment. Section one is the big idea: social engineering manipulates a person, not a machine. Section two is the heart of the day — the seven psychological tactics the College Board names, from pretexting to urgency, with a quick way to spot each. Section three is enrichment: real attacks stack these levers, so we'll pull apart one call that uses three at once.

[Slide 4]
Three things to walk out with today, identify social engineering attacks. First, name the seven tactics — pretexting, authority, intimidation, consensus, scarcity, familiarity, urgency — and the psychological lever each one pulls. Second, take any scenario — a call, an email, a text — and match it to the tactic in play, because that's exactly how the exam asks it. Third, explain why a real attack stacks several of these levers at once, and why the same fix — slow down and verify through a channel you trust — defeats every one. By the end of today you can read any scenario and label the lever the attacker is pulling.

[Slide 5]
Section one. Before we name the seven tactics, we have to understand the one idea underneath all of them: social engineering attacks the person. Then we get into the specific levers.

[Slide 6]
Two columns set up everything else. On the left, the big idea: social engineers use psychological tactics to manipulate targets into taking a desired action. Read that carefully — the target is a person, and the desired action is whatever the attacker wants, handing over a password, clicking a link, wiring money. No malware required; the human is the vulnerability. On the right, pretexting: adversaries create a believable reason to contact a target. The pretext is the cover story — ‘I'm from IT running a security audit.’ It's the opening move because a believable reason is what makes every tactic that follows actually work. The first is the why and the second is the setup.

[Slide 7]
Section two. Now the specific tactics — six psychological levers an attacker pulls once the pretext is in place. We'll take them in two grids of three. Watch for the one-line tell on each.

[Slide 8]
First grid of three — the pressure tactics, the ones that push a target to act fast. Authority: the adversary impersonates someone with power over you, a boss or an executive or an official, or pretends to be relaying that person's instructions, so you feel you have to comply. Intimidation: they state negative consequences if you don't do what they ask — you'll lose access, you'll be reported, something bad happens — fear drives the action. Urgency: they put you on a clock, a deadline that demands quick action to avoid a consequence, so you don't have time to stop and verify. Notice how naturally these three combine — that bell-ringer call used all three at once.

[Slide 9]
Second grid of three — the trust tactics, which lower your guard instead of raising your fear. Consensus: they create social pressure by making you believe everyone else is already doing the thing — ‘the whole team signed off, you're the last one’ — so you go along with the crowd. Scarcity: they manufacture limited availability, a one-time deal, only a few left, a window about to close, so you grab it before it's gone. Familiarity: they pretend to be or to know someone close to you to establish trust, borrowing a relationship you already have. Pressure tactics scare you into acting; trust tactics make you comfortable acting. Adversaries mix both.

[Slide 10]
Quick enrichment that pays off the bell ringer. Take that one help-desk call apart, line by line. ‘I'm from the IT help desk’ — that's the pretext, a believable reason to contact you. ‘Doing a required security audit’ — authority, posing as someone with power. ‘In the next ten minutes’ — urgency, a deadline that forces quick action. ‘Or your manager gets notified’ — intimidation, a stated negative consequence. Four tactics, one call. And they're stacked on purpose: the pretext keeps you on the line, authority makes refusing feel wrong, urgency strips away the time you'd use to call the real IT desk, and intimidation makes saying no feel costly. On the exam, don't stop at the first tactic — name every one. And notice the fix is identical for all of them: slow down and verify through a channel you trust, not the one the attacker handed you.

[Slide 11]
Eight terms, one for the big idea and seven for the tactics. Social engineering is the umbrella — manipulating a person, not a machine. Then the seven levers: pretexting is the believable reason to make contact; authority impersonates power; intimidation threatens consequences; consensus says everyone's already doing it; scarcity says it's almost gone; familiarity pretends a close relationship; urgency sets a deadline. Memorize these by name, because the exam gives you a scenario and asks you to name the tactic.

[Slide 12]
That gift-card email from the ‘CFO’ — name every tactic. You should find authority, posing as the CFO; urgency, the 30-minute deadline; and arguably intimidation or familiarity in the ‘don't loop anyone in.’ Be precise and cite. Then explain the difference between pressure tactics like intimidation and urgency and trust tactics like familiarity and consensus, and invent one example of each. This is exactly how the exam asks it.
