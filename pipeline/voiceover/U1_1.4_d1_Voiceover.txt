[Slide 1]
Lesson 1.4, AI-Based Cybersecurity Attacks. Everything you have learned so far — social engineering, password attacks, network attacks — still applies. What changes in this lesson is the tooling. Today, Day 1, covers the six specific ways the College Board says adversaries use AI-powered tools to make those attacks faster, cheaper, and far more convincing. Tomorrow, Day 2, covers how you defend against them, anchored on Scenario 1D. The danger today is not science fiction; it is that the old tells we relied on — the broken English in a phishing email, the certainty that a voice on the phone is really the person it claims to be — are exactly the things AI now removes.

[Slide 2]
Read the prompt: a call from your grandmother's actual number, in her actual voice, saying she is hurt and needs money wired immediately. In three to five sentences, tell me one reason you would believe it, and one way an attacker could fake a specific person's voice that well. Then: is there anything you could actually check in the moment? By the end of class you will know exactly how this is done — it is the first of six techniques today — and tomorrow you will know the one simple thing that defeats it.

[Slide 3]
Three sections today, all core except the last. Section one is AI aimed at people — deepfakes, AI-written phishing, and AI reconnaissance. Section two is attacks on and with AI systems themselves — pulling secrets out of a model, poisoning what a model learns, and using AI to write malicious code. Section three applies it all to Scenario 1D, the voice-cloning scam from your bell ringer.

[Slide 4]
One goal today, and it is broad: explain how adversaries use AI-powered tools to augment cyberattacks — The College Board names six distinct techniques under it. Notice the verb is explain, not just list. The AP exam will hand you a scenario and ask you to identify which AI technique is in play and explain how it makes the underlying attack work. By the end of today you should be able to do that for all six.

[Slide 5]
Section one. The first three techniques all point AI at a human target — they make deception more convincing and reconnaissance more thorough.

[Slide 6]
Three techniques, all aimed at a person. First: deepfake avatars. Feed an AI tool samples of someone's voice or face — easy to scrape from social media — and it can place a phone call or join a video call as that person. That is your bell ringer. And notice the sting in the tail: as more banks adopt voice authentication, a cloned voice is not just convincing, it can be the literal key. Second: AI phishing. For years we told you to look for broken grammar. An LLM writes flawless phishing in any language on demand, so that tell is dead. Third: AI reconnaissance. Tools that automatically scrape your social media and public footprint to build a profile of you — the research phase of an attack, now automated and at scale. Write all three.

[Slide 7]
Section two. The next three techniques turn on the AI systems themselves — pulling data out of a model, poisoning what a model learns, and using AI as a coding accomplice.

[Slide 8]
Three more, turned on the systems. First: prompt extraction. An LLM has seen sensitive things — other people's inputs, and whatever was in its training data — and a carefully crafted prompt can coax those secrets back out. Second: data poisoning. This one is subtle. The adversary plants false information on public websites specifically so it gets scraped into the next model's training set, and then the model confidently repeats the lie to everyone who asks. Third: AI-assisted coding. The same coding assistants that help developers help adversaries — writing new malware, weaponizing existing code, or scanning a huge codebase for the one exploitable bug. Write all three.

[Slide 9]
Here is the whole scenario as a chain. Step one is reconnaissance. The adversary sends a friend request under a name that looks like a kid from the target's school, gets accepted, and now has access to the target's video posts. They scrape clean voice samples and, from the same public posts, find a relative to target. Step two is the deepfake. Those voice samples go into a cloning tool, and the adversary calls the relative in the target's own voice with a manufactured emergency — arrested, in jail, wire bail money now. The relative does, because the voice sounded exactly like the target. Two techniques, one seamless attack. On the exam, name both links in the chain.

[Slide 10]
This is the most dangerous misconception in the whole lesson, because it creates false confidence. For a decade we taught two tells: AI voices sound robotic, and phishing has bad grammar. Both are now obsolete, and the College Board wrote the standard to say so. A cloning tool captures a specific person's accent and the exact way they say your name. An LLM produces phishing cleaner than most legitimate email. So if your whole defense is ‘I'll be able to tell,’ you have no defense — the better the AI, the more confident the wrong instinct feels. That is the bridge to tomorrow: every defense works WITHOUT you having to detect the fake, because detection is exactly what AI takes away from you.

[Slide 11]
Quick enrichment, because it explains the whole shape of tomorrow's lesson. On the left, the tells we used to teach: bad grammar means phishing, a familiar voice means the real person, a live video call can't be faked. Every one of those was reasonable five years ago. On the right, why AI erased each: native-fluent phishing on demand, voice cloning from a few public samples, avatar tools that fake a video call. Here is the takeaway: if detection is exactly the thing AI takes away from you, then a good defense can't depend on detection. That is why every defense tomorrow works without you ever having to tell real from fake.

[Slide 12]
Six terms, one per technique. Prioritize for the exam: digital avatar, native-fluent AI phishing, and AI reconnaissance — those are the three that show up most in scenarios. But know all six by name, because the exam can ask about any of them. If you see ‘the model repeated false information it had been trained on,’ that is poisoning. If you see ‘the model revealed sensitive data,’ that is prompt extraction. The distinction matters.

[Slide 13]
Question one: a deepfake video call impersonating a CFO. Name the technique, and explain why ‘but it looked and sounded like them’ is exactly the trust an attacker is exploiting. Question two distinguishes two techniques people confuse: planting false info that gets trained into a model — that is poisoning, information going IN — versus prompt extraction, information coming OUT. Be precise about the direction.
