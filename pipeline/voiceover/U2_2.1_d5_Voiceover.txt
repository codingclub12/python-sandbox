[Slide 1]
Lesson 2.1. The last few days built the risk picture: you can identify adversaries, trace the phases of an attack, and run the risk assessment process — figuring out which assets are exposed, how likely an attack is, and how severe the damage would be. Today is what an organization actually does with that information. The College Board is precise here: once a risk has been identified and assessed, an organization has exactly four options for managing it — avoid, transfer, mitigate, accept. That is the whole list. Today you'll learn what each one means, when each is possible, and why this is a business decision about cost and mission, not only a technical one. Tomorrow is the security controls that mitigation actually uses.

[Slide 2]
Your school's spirit-wear store has a checkout vulnerability that could leak customer card numbers. The store makes modest money, the fix is expensive, and insurance is available. In three to five sentences, list every distinct way the school could deal with this risk — and push past 'just fix it.' What other choices exist? By the end of class you'll know that the College Board says there are exactly four options, and you'll be able to name and apply each one.

[Slide 3]
Two sections today, both core. First, the four risk-management options themselves — avoid, transfer, mitigate, accept — what each means and when each is even possible. Second, the two ideas that make the framework realistic: residual risk, the leftover risk you accept after doing everything else, and cost-effectiveness, the reason organizations don't just mitigate everything.

[Slide 4]
Three things to leave with today, identify strategies for managing risk. First, name the four strategies — avoid, transfer, mitigate, accept — and know what each does: avoidance stops the risky activity, transference shifts the burden to an entity like an insurer, mitigation adds controls to shrink the risk, and acceptance is the deliberate choice to live with what's left. Second, pick the right strategy from a scenario, and explain why avoidance is off the table when the activity is critical to the mission. Third, define residual risk and use the cost-effectiveness test — favor controls that cost less than the expected loss — to judge when accepting or transferring beats mitigating. By the end of today you can name all four and apply them.

[Slide 5]
Section five of the lesson. Four options for managing a risk, and only four. As we go, listen for what makes each one possible — or impossible.

[Slide 6]
Three of the four strategies, side by side. First, avoid: you stop the activity generating the risk entirely — no checkout page, no card-theft risk. It's the cleanest option, but read the catch the College Board states: if the activity is critical to the organization's mission, avoidance isn't possible. A hospital can't avoid storing patient records. Second, transfer: the activity continues, but you push the burden onto another entity — an insurance company, a government, or consumers. Buying cyber-insurance is the textbook example. Third, mitigate: you keep doing the activity and you keep the risk, but you add security controls to reduce its likelihood or its impact. The fourth option, accept, is next — and it's different in kind.

[Slide 7]
The fourth option, acceptance, deserves its own slide because students get it wrong. On the left is residual risk: the risk that remains after an organization has gone through avoidance, transference, and mitigation. That leftover is the level of risk the organization is willing to accept — and risk acceptance is the acknowledgment that absolute security is unattainable. On the right is why acceptance has to exist: no control blocks every attack, and trying to eliminate every last risk would cost more than any organization could bear. So you name a level you can live with and you consciously accept it. The exam trap: acceptance is NOT ignoring the risk or doing nothing. It's the deliberate decision to operate with the residual risk after you've done everything cost-effective.

[Slide 8]
This is the misconception the exam loves. Students assume the only 'real' answer is mitigate-everything, and that accepting a risk means the team dropped the ball. But read the standard. It states flatly that absolute security is unattainable and residual risk always remains — so acceptance isn't a failure, it's built into the framework. And it adds the money lens: organizations favor solutions that are cost-effective and easy to maintain, where cost-effective means the control costs less than the expected loss it prevents. So pouring fifty thousand dollars into mitigating a few-hundred-dollar-a-year risk is actually the wrong decision. The right answer might be to mitigate the cheap fixes, transfer the rest with insurance, and accept the small remainder. The skill the College Board tests is matching the strategy to cost and mission — not always reaching for mitigation.

[Slide 9]
Six terms. The four strategies are the spine: avoidance stops the activity, transference shifts the burden to another entity, mitigation adds controls to shrink the risk, and acceptance is the deliberate choice to live with what's left. Then two supporting ideas the exam uses to make scenarios realistic: residual risk, the leftover after the first three, and cost-effective solution, one that costs less than the expected loss it prevents. Be able to tell avoidance from mitigation, because that's the pair students confuse.

[Slide 10]
Question one: the company shuts down the forum it doesn't need — name the strategy, that's avoidance, and explain why avoidance works here but not for the payment system the company can't live without. Question two is the integration question: the hospital encrypts and adds access controls, buys insurance, and documents what it chooses to live with — identify all three strategies, mitigation, transference, and acceptance, and define the residual risk it's accepting. Be precise about which strategy is which.
