VOICEOVER SCRIPT — Lesson 1.3-day3 · Best Practices for Public Networks
============================================================

[Slide 1]
Lesson 1.3. Today you move from analyst to consultant. Yesterday you identified the attacks. Today three real clients need your advice on what to actually DO about public Wi-Fi. The Scenario 1C coffee shop is Client 1. Recommendations must cite a specific defense. Vague 'be careful' answers score zero.

[Slide 2]
Consider three login portals. For each one, decide — real or fake — and name the specific red flag. The goal is to build the instinct so fast that walking into any coffee shop you'd catch it in under ten seconds. Real coffee shops almost never ask for a social media login. A URL that doesn't match the venue name is almost always a giveaway. An SSL warning is a five-alarm fire.

[Slide 3]
Here is the rubric in one sentence. Every recommendation needs three things: the specific threat, the specific defense by name, and one line explaining why that defense addresses this client's exact risk. A single defense answer is almost never the full-credit answer when multiple defenses apply. That is the AP pattern.

[Slide 4]
Work through all three clients in order. On Client 2 there's a VPN trade-off question about whether a company should run their own VPN or use a consumer service. There's no single right answer — what matters is the reasoning. On Client 3 the risk matrix is the key part — don't just say HIGH or LOW, name the defense.

[Slide 5]
We're walking through all three. For each client, consider the attack identification, then the defense recommendation. The trap on each one is where most points get dropped — pay attention to those.

[Slide 6]
Client 1, the coffee shop. What was the attack? Evil twin. What was Jamie's specific mistake? Two things — didn't verify the SSID, AND entered social media credentials into a portal on an untrusted network. The shop recommendations: post the exact SSID prominently, and drop any third-party login requirement from the portal. The customer safety card needs to map to specific defenses. Watch the trap: VPN is a customer defense, not a shop fix. The shop can't install VPNs on customers' devices.

[Slide 7]
Client 2, remote workers. Likely attack: evil twin in the hotel. Hotels are the highest-risk venue because guests expect login portals and are time-pressured. The three policy rules map one-to-one to the three defenses — that's the design. The VPN trade-off question is the hardest one: the standard is explicit that the VPN provider can VIEW traffic. A company-run VPN shifts trust to the employer, who already has authorized access to company data. A consumer VPN shifts trust to a stranger. Full credit requires naming who you trust and why.

[Slide 8]
Client 3 is the most practical one for students because Taylor is their age. The risk matrix is four points — emphasis on justification. Email and Venmo are high risk because they involve credential entry and financial transactions. Instagram and Netflix use HTTPS and don't require entering sensitive credentials in a risky way, so low risk. The SSID habit is the most transferable skill in this whole lesson: find the posted name, compare exactly, when uncertain use cellular. That's the defense simplified for a real person.

[Slide 9]
Here's a question worth real arguments, not safe answers. Which one defense has the biggest real-world impact for a typical person who isn't a security professional? SSID verification is the highest-leverage because it prevents the most common attack before you're even connected. VPN is the most powerful once connected but requires trusting a new party. Protocol awareness is the most passive. The follow-up is the one worth arguing: the VPN trade-off. The standard is blunt about it — the VPN provider can view your traffic. Is a VPN company more trustworthy than a coffee shop Wi-Fi operator? There's no right answer. The exam tests that you know the trade-off exists.

[Slide 10]
Three patterns to remember before tomorrow's quiz. One: layered defense. The exam rarely wants just one defense. Two: match the defense to the specific threat mechanism — wrong defense, no credit. Three: know the trade-offs. The VPN trade-off is explicitly in the standard. If a question mentions that someone used a VPN but was still compromised, you need to know why.

[Slide 11]
Day 4 is the quiz. Five AP-style questions, timed at about three minutes per question, which is actual AP exam pacing. Full debrief after. Review tonight: the three attack types, the three defenses, and the trade-offs — especially the HTTPS-doesn't-stop-credential-harvesting point from Day 2 and the VPN-shifts-trust point from today. Those are the two highest-probability exam traps in this lesson.
