[Slide 1]
Lesson 2.3, Protecting Spaces. In Topic 2.2 you learned to find physical vulnerabilities and assess the risk they create. Topic 2.3 is the answer to that risk: the controls that protect a physical space. We have four class periods. Today, Day 1, covers managerial controls, which are the policies and people-focused measures that protect a workplace. Days 2 and 3 are the physical controls and how to choose them, with Day 3 applying them to Scenario 2A, Securing Xtensr Labs. Day 4 is review and practice. Today, two things: employee security awareness training, and the workstation security policy. Notice the word managerial — these are not fences and locks, they are rules and training. That distinction is exactly what the exam tests.

[Slide 2]
You badge into a restricted lab, and a friendly stranger asks you to hold the door because they ‘forgot their badge’ — and meanwhile a coworker walked off leaving their computer unlocked with a client list sitting on the desk. Name two things that just went wrong, and for each, give a rule or a habit that would have stopped it — not a fence, not a lock, a rule or a habit. Both problems here are solved by managerial controls — training and policy — and that is exactly today's learning objective.

[Slide 3]
Three sections today, two core and one enrichment. Section one is the first managerial control, security awareness training — teaching people to spot phishing, to stop tailgating, and to prevent device theft. Section two is the second managerial control, the workstation security policy, with its four common requirements. Section three is a short enrichment that names the bigger picture: today is managerial controls, rules and training, while Days 2 and 3 are physical controls, fences and locks. Telling those two categories apart is a tested skill, so I want it in your notes.

[Slide 4]
Three objectives today — identify managerial controls related to physical security. First, identify the two managerial controls the College Board names — security awareness training and the workstation security policy — and the three behaviors training targets: detecting phishing, not badging others in, and preventing device theft. Second, explain what a workstation security policy requires — locking devices, a clean desk, a privacy screen filter, a surge protector or UPS — and what the word tiers means. Third, distinguish a managerial control from a physical control when a measure is described, because the exam loves to disguise a policy or a training program as if it were a lock or a gate. By the end of today you can do all three.

[Slide 5]
Section one, the first of two managerial controls: employee security awareness training. The idea is simple but powerful — your people are a control.

[Slide 6]
Here is the first managerial control: organizations should conduct employee security awareness training to educate employees about how they can contribute to the organization's security. The College Board names three specific behaviors. One, detecting social engineering attempts like phishing — the employee learns to spot the trick email. Two, not badging other people into restricted areas — that is the door-holding stranger from your bell ringer, called tailgating, and the trained employee politely refuses. Three, preventing device theft — habits like never leaving a laptop unattended. On the right, why this counts as managerial: no fence or lock catches a tailgater the way a trained person does, and phishing slips past technical controls by targeting the human. Managerial controls direct people through rules and training. Write down all three behaviors.

[Slide 7]
Section two. Same objective, the second managerial control: the workstation security policy. This is the rulebook for what happens at and around an employee's desk.

[Slide 8]
The second managerial control: organizations should have a workstation security policy that outlines the measures necessary to protect a physical workplace. Two things to note before the list. First, the policy may have tiers of workstation security based on the type of data handled at a workstation — a desk handling medical records gets stricter rules than a lobby kiosk. Second, here are the common requirements. Lock devices before leaving them unattended to prevent unauthorized access, and clear sensitive documents off the desk — that is the clean desk policy. Use a privacy screen filter or other physical barrier so no one can read the screen over your shoulder. And connect devices to surge protectors or an uninterruptible power supply, a UPS, to protect against power problems. Four requirements, one policy. Write down the requirements, and write the word tiers.

[Slide 9]
Here is the misconception built into how the objective is worded. The phrase ‘physical security’ makes students assume the controls must be physical objects — locks, fences, cameras — and that training and policy are just paperwork. But read the objective: it says identify MANAGERIAL controls related to physical security. Managerial means the rules and training that direct people, and the College Board lists them first in Topic 2.3, before any hardware. Why do they matter so much? Picture the best card readers money can buy, and one untrained employee holds the door for a stranger — defeated instantly. The other direction: a clean desk policy and a locked screen stop someone already inside from reading sensitive data, which no fence can do. So when a question describes training or a desk policy, the category is managerial. Do not let the word ‘physical’ trick you into calling it a physical control.

[Slide 10]
Quick enrichment, because the sorting is worth points. On the left, today's managerial controls: security awareness training and the workstation security policy. The thing they have in common is that the control is a decision a person makes or a habit they keep — and they're defeated by a careless human, not a cut wire. On the right, the physical controls coming in Days 2 and 3: fences, gates, bollards, locks, card readers, vestibules, turnstiles, disabled USB ports, UPS and generators. Those are objects that do their job whether or not anyone was trained. Both halves are ‘physical security,’ but the College Board splits the controls into managerial — people and policy — and physical — hardware. On the exam, name the category first, then the specific control. That two-part answer is what scores.

[Slide 11]
Six terms, anchored on the two key ideas. The big organizing word is managerial control — a control made of rules and training, not hardware. Know security awareness training and the behavior it stops, tailgating, which is following or being let into a restricted area without badging. Know the workstation security policy and two of its named pieces: the clean desk policy, clearing sensitive documents, and the privacy screen filter, which blocks shoulder-surfing. Know all six by name — and for each, be ready to say it's a managerial control.

Question two: a hospital wanting stricter rules for record-handling desks than for the lobby kiosk — that's the workstation security policy, the idea is tiers based on the type of data handled, and give me two named requirements like locking devices or the clean desk policy.
