Welcome back to Lesson 2.4. Yesterday, you learned the four ways security controls detect a physical attack — cameras, guards, motion sensors, and employees. But a detection control is only as good as where you put it. Today is: determining the effective placement of detection and monitoring equipment. We'll cover what to consider when placing cameras — coverage, angle, tamper-resistance, and ingress and egress points; where motion sensors belong and where they create so many false alarms they become useless; where locks and access control vestibules go; and the difference between stationary and patrolling guards. The recurring idea today is that placement is what turns equipment into actual detection — the same sensor is invaluable in a server room and worthless in a busy lobby.

You have one motion sensor and two spots: the busy main lobby, or the normally-empty server room only two people may enter. Where does it go, and why? And what specifically goes wrong if you put it in the other spot? The phrase 'false alarm' belongs in the answer. This is almost word for word — the College Board says motion sensors belong where traffic is unexpected, not where everyone walks, and the reason is exactly the false-alarm problem you're about to write about.

Section two covers the other three controls' placement: motion sensors where traffic is unexpected, locks and access control vestibules on sensitive areas, and stationary versus patrolling guards. Three objectives today — determine effective placement of security controls. Notice the verb changed from yesterday: yesterday was identify the methods, today is determine where each one goes. First, place a camera using the four considerations the standard names — coverage, angle, tamper-resistance, and what the camera could capture an adversary doing — and remember points of ingress and egress are often monitored by camera. Second, place a motion sensor where traffic is unexpected, and explain why putting one in a high-traffic area breeds false alarms that train people to ignore the alarm. [Slide 5]
Section two of the lesson. Start with cameras, because their placement has the most moving parts — coverage, angle, tampering, and what the shot actually captures.

[Slide 6]
Camera placement is four deliberate considerations, not 'point it at the room.' On the left: first, visual coverage — does it actually see the area that matters? Second, angle — a camera aimed too high catches the tops of heads and no faces; the angle has to make the footage useful. Third, the ability to be tampered with — can an adversary reach up and block it, turn it, spray it, or cut power? Mount it out of reach and protect it. Fourth, and this is the subtle one the standard calls out: think about what a camera in this specific spot could capture an adversary doing, and how that information would be helpful. On the right is the highest-value placement: points of ingress and egress — ways in and ways out — are often monitored by camera, because everyone entering or leaving funnels past a doorway, the perfect chokepoint to capture identity and timing. Write the four considerations and the ingress/egress rule.

[Slide 7]
Section three. Same objective. Now the other three controls — motion sensors, locks and vestibules, and the two kinds of guards. Each has a placement rule the exam loves.

[Slide 8]
Three placement rules. First, motion sensors go where traffic is unexpected — server rooms, storage for sensitive materials, places few people may enter — because there, ANY movement is itself the alarm. Second, the flip side, and this is the one the exam tests: motion sensors in high-traffic areas create many false alarms, and those false alarms train people to ignore the alarm, so when a real event finally happens, no one takes it seriously. A sensor in the wrong place is worse than no sensor. Third on stationary guards: they give constant protection to a specific area, entrance, or high-value item, and they're highly effective at places that funnel traffic — entry gates, main entrances, lobbies, the doors to more secure areas. Write all three. We'll cover patrolling guards and locks on the next slide.

[Slide 9]
Two more placements. On the left, locks and vestibules: locks go on ALL entries to areas with sensitive information or systems, and for the most sensitive areas, an access control vestibule at the entry point — that's the two-door airlock from Topic 2.3 — to prevent piggybacking and tailgating, so one badge lets exactly one person through. On the right, the other half of patrolling guards: unlike a stationary guard fixed at a chokepoint, a patrolling guard is harder for an adversary to plan around because their rounds are unpredictable, which creates time pressure — the adversary doesn't know when the guard returns. Patrolling guards are better for perimeters and exterior areas. So the rule of thumb: stationary guards for chokepoints that funnel traffic, patrolling guards for perimeters and open ground. Write both placements.

[Slide 10]
This is the placement misconception the exam loves, and it's a trap because it sounds reasonable: more sensors, more coverage, more safety. But read. In high-traffic areas, motion sensors create many false alarms, and here's the human consequence the standard spells out — those false alarms make people stop taking the alarm seriously, so when a real event happens, nobody responds. A sensor in a busy lobby fires hundreds of times a day on ordinary employees; within a week security tunes it out as noise, and the one night an adversary trips it, the alarm gets ignored because it has always meant nothing. The exact same sensor in the empty server room would have screamed 'intruder.' Same device, opposite value, and the only difference is placement. So the rule isn't 'more sensors' — it's sensors where traffic is unexpected, so every alarm is worth answering. Write the cause: false alarms erode trust in the alarm.

[Slide 11]
Quick enrichment to tie together. Every placement rule in this objective is really the same idea: placement is what turns a device into detection. On the left, well-placed controls — a doorway camera that captures every face, a server-room sensor where any movement matters, a stationary guard screening the main entrance, a vestibule that stops tailgating. On the right, the identical devices placed wrong — a camera angled at the ceiling that captures no faces, a lobby sensor buried in false alarms, a patrolling guard on a predictable schedule an adversary can time, a camera mounted low enough to simply cover. And here's the danger that's worse than no control: a poorly placed control creates false confidence. You think you're protected, you stop watching, and you're actually more exposed than if you'd installed nothing. That's why placement exists as its own objective — owning the equipment isn't enough; placing it correctly is the skill.

[Slide 12]
Six terms. The camera pair — visual coverage and points of ingress and egress — are the ideas the exam tests, so know that ingress means ways in and egress means ways out. False alarm is the single most important term today: it's the reason motion sensors don't go in busy areas. Access control vestibule carries over from Topic 2.3 and is the answer for the most sensitive entries. And know the stationary-versus-patrolling guard distinction — stationary for chokepoints, patrolling for perimeters. All six.
