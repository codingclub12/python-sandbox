[Slide 6]
Here's why physical risk gets its own objective. The standard says physical access to devices can allow adversaries to bypass many technical controls and layers of security. Think about what a firewall, passwords, and encryption actually do: they defend a device from attacks arriving over the network. Now put an adversary physically in front of that device. The firewall is irrelevant — they're already inside the perimeter. They can unplug it, pull the drive, plug in a keylogger, or walk out with it, exactly the harms from yesterday. So a single unlocked door can undo a huge investment in digital security. That's the reason we assess physical risk separately, and it's why a physical vulnerability can rate high even at an organization with excellent network security. Remember the phrase 'bypass technical controls.'

[Slide 8]
The three risk levels, with the College Board's own illustrative examples — these are worth memorizing. High risk: sensitive information or systems exposed in physical spaces without sufficiently restricted and controlled access. The example is the server with customer data in a room with no lock, off an unmonitored hallway — sensitive asset, wide-open access. Moderate risk: a noncritical or nonsensitive part of the organization left unprotected in a way that could act as a foothold to reach other resources. The example is the reception computer with exposed USB ports that's on the internal Wi-Fi — not sensitive itself, but a stepping stone inward. Low risk: a low-value asset where exploitation is unlikely. The example is badge-access employees leaving non-sensitive laptops out during a group lunch — low value, behind a badge, unlikely to be exploited. Write all three definitions and examples. Notice the two dials: how sensitive the asset is, and how likely the exploit.

[Slide 9]
Let's read the three examples as one method. Every grade turns on two dials: how sensitive or valuable the exposed asset is, and how reachable — how likely to be exploited — it is. Top row, high: the asset is sensitive, a server with customer data, and access is wide open, no lock and an unmonitored hallway. Sensitive plus open equals high. Middle row, moderate: the asset itself is noncritical — a reception computer — but it's a foothold, exposed USB ports on a machine that's on the internal network, so an adversary could use it to reach bigger resources. Bottom row, low: the asset is low value, non-sensitive laptops, and exploitation is unlikely because they're behind badge access during a brief group lunch. So the method is two questions: how sensitive is the asset, and how likely is the exploit. Hold those two dials in your head — you'll turn them on every vulnerability at Xtensr tomorrow.

[Slide 10]
Here's the misconception that derails risk assessment. After two days of scary attacks and harms, students want to stamp everything 'high' and fix it all now. But the entire purpose of the standard is that risk is not uniform — it's graded by two factors, the sensitivity or value of what's exposed and how likely it is to be exploited. The College Board's own examples make the point: the unlocked server room full of customer data is high, but the badge-protected laptops left out during lunch are low. Treat them the same and you'd send your team to cable down cheap laptops while the customer-data server sits unlocked. That's backwards. Documenting risk levels is exactly how a team prioritizes when it can't fix everything at once — and that prioritization is the skill the exam and the Xtensr scenario test.
