[Slide 1]
Welcome back to Lesson 2.1, Cyber Foundations. You've learned the tactics, and the adversaries. Today, you learn the choreography — the phases a cyberattack moves through. The College Board names six: reconnaissance, initial access, persistence, lateral movement, taking action, and evading detection. Two things to lock in from the start. First, these are in order — the sequence matters and the exam tests it. Second, not every attack uses every phase. Today you'll learn to read an incident, name each phase, and put them in the right order.

[Slide 2]
Here's an investigator's report with six attacker actions listed out of order: exported the customer database, logged in with a stolen password, researched employees on LinkedIn, deleted the server logs, installed malware to stay connected, and used an admin account to reach the finance server. Put them in the order they actually happened. What has to come first? What comes last, and why? Every one of those actions is a named phase in today's standard, and getting the order right is exactly the skill the exam tests.

[Slide 3]
Two sections, both core, and together they cover all six phases in order. Section one is getting in and staying in: reconnaissance to scout the target, initial access to break in, and persistence to keep the door open. Section two is spreading, acting, and hiding: lateral movement to escalate privileges, taking action to hit the objective, and evading detection to cover tracks. The order is half the points on this objective.

[Slide 4]
Three things to leave with today, describe the phases of a cyberattack. First, name each of the six phases — reconnaissance, initial access, persistence, lateral movement, taking action, evading detection — from a description of what an adversary did. Second, put them in the correct order and explain why each phase sets up the next, since the sequence is half the points. Third, apply the College Board's caveat that not every attack uses every phase — identify only the phases that are actually present rather than forcing all six. By the end of today you can read an incident and both name and order the phases.

[Slide 5]
Section one. The first three phases — scout, break in, dig in. As we go, notice how each phase sets up the next.

[Slide 6]
First three phases, in order. Phase one, reconnaissance: the adversary gathers everything they can about the target, often using open source intelligence — OSINT — which is just freely available information from public websites, social media, public records. No break-in yet; this is scouting. Phase two, initial access: they establish a foothold on the target's computer, often through social engineering — there's yesterday's and Day 1's material — or through compromised or weak credentials. Phase three, persistence: now they make sure they can get back in without breaking in again, often using a command and control, or C2, protocol to send commands and receive output, usually through malware like a remote access trojan, a RAT, or a rootkit. Note the key term in each.

[Slide 7]
Section two. Phases four through six — spread, strike, and clean up. And remember the standard's caveat: not every attack uses every phase.

[Slide 8]
The last three phases, in order. Phase four, lateral movement: from their initial foothold, adversaries try to escalate privileges by reaching computers and user accounts with elevated permissions — admin accounts, service accounts — that get them to the data they actually want. Phase five, taking action: this is the payoff, where they act on their objectives — collecting targeted data and exfiltrating it, disrupting services, or destroying data. This is where the real damage happens. Phase six, evading detection: the cleanup, where many adversaries remove or edit log files and erase other files they planted, like malware, so the defenders never see how it happened. Now you have all six in order.

[Slide 9]
Here's the misconception the exam loves. Because we teach the phases as a numbered one-through-six list, students assume every attack must hit all six in order — and that if you can't find, say, lateral movement, you've made a mistake. But read: the phases may not all be used in every attack. A quick data grab might skip persistence and lateral movement. A hacktivist defacing a website may do a little recon, get access, deface it — that's taking action — and stop, with no persistence, no lateral movement, and no evading detection, because being seen is the whole point. The order is the typical arc, not a checklist every attack completes. On the exam, identify the phases that are present and order those — don't force all six onto an attack that only used three.

[Slide 10]
Seven terms — the six phases plus OSINT. Memorize the phases in order: reconnaissance, initial access, persistence, lateral movement, taking action, evading detection. For each, hold the one-line description, because the exam describes the action and asks the phase. OSINT is the term to know inside reconnaissance — freely available information. And note the connections: initial access often uses the social engineering from earlier this week, and persistence uses the malware terms RAT and rootkit.

[Slide 11]
Take the six bell-ringer actions, label each with its phase name, and put them in the correct order — researching employees is reconnaissance, stolen-password login is initial access, malware to stay connected is persistence, admin account to the finance server is lateral movement, exporting the database is taking action, deleting logs is evading detection. Then explain why recon has to come before access. And: a ransomware group breaks in, encrypts everything, demands payment, and doesn't hide — which phases did they use, which did they skip, the not-every-attack caveat. Be precise.
