Today is all about consequences — describing the possible impacts for victims of social engineering. We break it into three targets. First, name the three things a victim can give up: personal information, a secure code like a one-time password, or a click or download that hands over access. Second, explain how each of those becomes a bigger problem — personal details feed impersonation and account challenge questions, a secure code lets an adversary log in as the victim, and a malicious click or download leads to malware or a credential-capture site. Third, you’ll practice taking any successful attack and naming its most likely impact, then defending that choice with the standard. By the end you should be able to connect what the victim lost to what the adversary gained.

Two sections today, both core. First, what victims can lose — the three impact categories the standard names. Second, we connect each impact to what the adversary gains, because that is exactly how the exam frames its questions.

[Slide 5]
Section three, and our whole focus today. The College Board groups the possible impacts into three categories. Get these three and you can answer any impact question on this topic.

[Slide 6]
Three categories. One: personal information — names, phone numbers, addresses, workplaces, pets' names, birthdates. Why does that matter? Because those are the exact facts websites use as challenge questions to verify who you are, so giving them up enables impersonation. Two: a login code — if you read out a one-time password or authentication code, the adversary uses it to log in to your account as you. Three: a click or a download — clicking a link or downloading a file can install malware, steal what's in your browser, or drop you on a fake page that captures your credentials. Personal info, a login code, a click. Three buckets.

[Slide 7]
Here's the move the exam actually wants. It rarely asks “what is the impact” in a vacuum — it asks what the adversary can DO with what the victim gave up. So practice the middle column. Give up a pet's name and a birthday, and the adversary gains answers to your challenge questions, a path to impersonation. Give up an OTP, and they gain a live login as you. Click a malicious link, and they gain installed malware, stolen browser data, or your credentials captured on a fake page. And look — yesterday's bell ringer lands right here in rows two and three: the teacher's typed password was captured on a fake page, which hands the adversary a login, the same end state as giving up a code.

[Slide 8]
Three moves cover almost every Topic 1.1 question. Move one: name the tactic. See a threat? Intimidation. See a deadline or time pressure? Urgency. Those are the only two named tactics here, so you're never choosing from a long list. Move two: explain the instinct — fear of consequences for intimidation, reacting fast under pressure for urgency. Move three: trace the impact — turn what the victim gave up into what the adversary can now do. Tactic, instinct, impact. Practice those three and this topic is yours.

[Slide 11]
Lesson 1.1 in one page. Social engineering manipulates people, not technology, to reveal information, download a file, or click a link. Two named tactics: intimidation, the threat of a negative consequence, and urgency, a deadline that forces fast action. They work because they exploit instincts everyone has — fear and time pressure. And the impacts come in three: personal info leads to impersonation, a login code leads to account takeover, and a click leads to malware or credential capture.
