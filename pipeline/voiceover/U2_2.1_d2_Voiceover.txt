[Slide 1]
Welcome back to Lesson 2.1, Cyber Foundations. Yesterday, you learned how social engineers manipulate a person. Today, you learn who is on the other end of those attacks — the five types of adversaries the College Board names, and what drives each one. This matters because motivation predicts behavior: a script kiddie chasing recognition behaves nothing like a transnational criminal organization chasing profit, and an insider with a badge is a different kind of threat entirely. Know the adversary and you can anticipate the attack.

[Slide 2]
Three breaches in one week: a teenager defaces a school site with a tool he can't explain; a departing employee copies the customer database on his way out; a group locks a hospital's files for ransom. In three to five sentences, tell me what each attacker actually wanted. Are they all after the same thing? And what makes the employee fundamentally different from the other two? Each of these is a named adversary type in today's standard, and the thing that separates them is motivation and access.

[Slide 3]
Two sections today, both core. First, the lower-resource adversaries: script kiddies defined by low skill, hacktivists defined by a cause, and insiders defined by legitimate access. Second, the organized and state-level adversaries: cyberterrorists who target whole communities, and transnational criminal organizations chasing money at scale. For every type, the College Board gives you a motivation — that's the column to fill carefully, because the exam tests adversary by motivation.

[Slide 4]
Three things to leave with today, identify types of adversaries. First, name the five types — script kiddie, hacktivist, insider, cyberterrorist, transnational criminal organization — and pin each to its defining motivation: recognition or greed, a cause, revenge with access, disruption of communities, or financial gain at scale. Second, take a breach description and match it to the right adversary using motive and target, the way the exam asks. Third, explain why the insider is called a unique threat, and why motivation and access — not raw skill — decide how dangerous an adversary really is. By the end of today you can read a breach and name the adversary.

[Slide 5]
Section one. The first three adversary types — defined not by big budgets but by skill level, by a cause, and by access.

[Slide 6]
Three adversaries, each defined by one distinctive thing. Script kiddies: low-skilled, they run tools other people built without understanding how those tools work, and they're usually after greed or recognition — bragging rights. Hacktivists: motivated by a social, political, or personal cause, they compromise systems to support that cause or stop harm they perceive, and crucially they believe their goals justify breaking the law. Insider adversaries: the unique one, because they already have legitimate credentials and access — they don't have to break in. They can be recruited by outsiders and are often driven by greed or revenge. The one word that defines each: skill, cause, access.

[Slide 7]
Section two. The two highest-impact adversaries — one aiming to disrupt whole communities, the other to profit at scale.

[Slide 8]
Two columns, two motives. On the left, cyberterrorists: driven by politics or beliefs, they want to disrupt entire communities, regions, or nations, which is why their targets are civil infrastructure — power grids, water treatment plants. And they can act on their own or on behalf of a government or criminal organization. On the right, transnational criminal organizations: pure financial gain, primarily by deploying ransomware to extort victims and stealing corporate intellectual property to sell in illegal markets. Both are serious, but the tell is the motive — disruption of a community versus money. Match the target to the motive and you'll name the adversary every time.

[Slide 9]
Here's the misconception, and the exam leans on it. We imagine a hacker as a genius coder, so the low-skill script kiddie and the ordinary employee seem harmless. But read the standard: the College Board defines these adversaries by motivation and access, not just skill. A script kiddie with a downloaded ransomware kit can lock a clinic's files cold — and remember from the risk lessons that well-documented exploits lower the skill bar so more people can pull them off. The insider is the sharpest example: the standard literally calls them a unique threat, not because they're skilled but because they already have legitimate credentials and access — no firewall stops a valid login. So when a scenario describes a ‘basic’ attacker or a trusted employee, don't dismiss them. Motive plus access is the threat.

[Slide 10]
Six terms. The five adversary types are the core — script kiddie, hacktivist, insider adversary, cyberterrorist, transnational criminal organization — and for each, the motivation is what you memorize, because that's how the exam asks. The sixth, ransomware, is the signature tool of the transnational criminal organization, and you'll meet it again in the attack-phases lesson. Pair each adversary with its one-word motive.

[Slide 11]
A power grid goes down, the attackers cite a political grievance, no ransom — name the adversary, that's a cyberterrorist, and explain how the infrastructure target and the political motive give it away. Then the heart of today: why does the standard call insiders a unique threat? Contrast an insider with an equally skilled outside script kiddie — who's more dangerous to confidential data and why? The answer is access. Be precise.
