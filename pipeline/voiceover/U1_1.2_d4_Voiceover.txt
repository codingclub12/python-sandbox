[Slide 6]
Let's walk through all five questions. The answer key is C, A, D, B, A.

[Slide 7]
Q1. Answer C — I and II only. The I/II/III format tests whether students can apply each sign precisely. Entry I is textbook Sign 1 — 23 failures in 4 minutes is automated, not human. Entry II hits Signs 2 and 3 simultaneously — off-hours plus never-visited country. Entry III is the trap: one mistype then success is exactly what a legitimate user does when they fumble their password. That is not Sign 1. Students who picked D missed the distinction. Remember: volume plus speed equals Sign 1. One failure is not Sign 1.

[Slide 8]
Q2. Answer A. The question asks which password most directly matches the standard's pattern — word plus two-digit year plus special character at end. P@ssword2024! Is the exact formula. The @ substitution for the letter a is also a predictable variation adversaries account for. Qwerty123 is obviously weak but does not match that specific pattern. Correct-horse-battery-staple is actually strong — a passphrase. Xk#9mQpLv2 looks random and is harder to crack than any of the others. The exam tests whether students know the specific named pattern, not just general password weakness.

[Slide 9]
Q3. Answer D. The inversion is the trap — LEAST likely, not most likely. Students who identified Biscuit2015 as the weakest password answered the wrong question. The OSINT dictionary is built from what the adversary found: Biscuit, March 15, Chiefs. Any combination of those three pieces of information plus common patterns appears in the dictionary. Xm7#kR9vLq2p has no connection to any of that. An adversary cannot construct it from personal information. It would only appear in a brute-force attack, not a targeted dictionary attack. This question tests whether students understand how the dictionary is constructed — from personal info, not from random guessing.

[Slide 10]
Q4. Answer B. I and II only. This is one of the highest-leverage questions on this quiz because it tests whether students know what the standard actually says about authentication, not just general security lore. Forced password rotation sounds like good practice — change your password often, use special characters — but that is not what the standard says. The standard says long, random, unique, password manager, and MFA. Complexity rules and rotation schedules are not in the standard. In fact, NIST guidance — which is enrichment context for this lesson — explicitly discourages forced rotation because users respond to it by making small predictable increments. That is the enrichment answer. For the AP exam, you need to know: rotation is not a specified strengthening method.

[Slide 11]
Q5. Answer A. This question is set in the Scenario 1B context your students analyzed on Day 2 — the same log they worked through. Changing the password is correct. But that is only one of three controls the standard specifies. MFA is the control that makes it hardest for the adversary to log in again even if they somehow obtain the new password. B is wrong because the standard names MFA as a separate required strengthening method. C has no basis in the standard at all, and D — forced rotation — deliberately echoes Q4: rotation is never a specified method. The point: on a free-response question, naming one defense when the scenario calls for complete remediation will cost points. The exam expects students to think across all three items.
