[Slide 6]
Here is the method: to determine a relevant control, a cyber defender considers how an adversary could take advantage of a vulnerability to attack a system, and how to prevent, detect, or correct the attack. Two moves. On the left, think like the adversary — how would they actually exploit this gap? You can't pick a good control until you understand the attack, and starting from the attack instead of a catalog keeps your control relevant. On the right, decide what the control should do: prevent the attack from happening, like a lock or a fence; detect it when it occurs, like a card-reader log; or correct it, limit or recover from the damage, like a UPS keeping a device alive through a power cut. Every control you'll meet today fits at least one of those three. Write the three words: prevent, detect, correct.

[Slide 7]
Section two. Same objective. Now the toolkit — the specific physical controls the College Board names. As each one comes up, ask: what does it prevent, detect, or correct?

[Slide 8]
Here's the core of the toolkit. First, the perimeter: fencing, gates, and bollards around a building deter adversaries from even trying to get physical access — and bollards specifically stop vehicles, which answers the parking-lot-meets-glass-wall vulnerability from your bell ringer. Second, locks: locks on doors, server cabinets, and computers prevent devices from being accessed or stolen — that's the answer to the unlocked server cabinet. Third, entry control, two ideas together: card readers, record which badge opened which entry at what time and deny unauthorized badges — note that's both detection and prevention. And access control vestibules and turnstiles, stop an authorized person from letting an unauthorized person in, on purpose or by accident — that's the hardware answer to tailgating. Write all of these and what each one prevents or detects.

[Slide 9]
Two more controls that protect the machine itself. On the left: organizations can disable USB ports to prevent external drives from loading malware onto a computer. That closes a physical data path — even if someone gets to the keyboard, they can't plug in an infected drive. It's a preventive control. On the right: an uninterruptible power supply, a UPS, provides backup power for a device during an outage, and power generators do the same at a larger scale for a building or set of critical devices. Notice this one is corrective — it doesn't stop the outage, it limits the loss by keeping critical devices running. Map each to the prevent-detect-correct model: USB disabling prevents, UPS and generators correct. Write both.

[Slide 10]
Here's the trap on Day 2. Every control sounds protective, so students grab the most impressive one and skip the reasoning. But the standard says match the control to the specific attack — figure out how the adversary exploits this particular vulnerability, then pick a control that prevents, detects, or corrects that attack. Bollards do nothing for an unlocked server cabinet; a UPS does nothing about tailgating. And the standard reminds you that organizations prioritize by severity and cost, so piling on controls isn't the goal. Picture recommending a building-wide generator to fix a propped-open door — wrong and wasteful; that door needs a lock and maybe a vestibule. Meanwhile a twenty-dollar lock can beat a fifty-thousand-dollar perimeter upgrade for a theft risk. On the exam, name the control that fits the attack, justify it with prevent-detect-correct, and weigh it against severity and cost.

[Slide 11]
Quick enrichment, and it's required by the standard. Once you've found controls for every vulnerability, you still have to decide what to fund, because the budget is finite. The College Board names two factors: the severity of the risk and the cost of the mitigation. On the left, those two factors — a severe risk with a cheap fix is the easiest yes, fund it first; a minor risk with an expensive fix might be deferred or accepted. On the right, why it matters: you can't fix everything at once, so ranking by severity and cost decides the order, and that connects straight back to the risk assessment you did in Topic 2.2. On the exam, when you're asked to prioritize, use both factors — severity and cost — not just one. Write down both factors.
