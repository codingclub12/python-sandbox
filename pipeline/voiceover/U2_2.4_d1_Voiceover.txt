Lesson 2.4, Detecting Attacks. Topic 2.3 was about protecting a physical space — the managerial controls and mitigation strategies that keep adversaries out. But no control is perfect, so this lesson is about the next layer: detection, noticing that an attack is happening or has happened. Today, Day 1, covers — the four specific ways the College Board says security controls can detect a physical attack: cameras, security guards, motion sensors, and the employees who work in the space. Tomorrow, Day 2, is where we place that equipment effectively. Day 3 applies all of it to Scenario 2A, Securing Xtensr Labs. Day 4 is review and practice for the whole topic. Notice across today's four detection methods a recurring idea: detection only matters if someone or something is actually watching and can respond. A camera no one monitors, a sensor no one answers — those detect nothing. Keep that in mind.

The lab has hallway cameras, a motion sensor in the server room, a guard at the front lobby, and employees who work in nearby offices during the day. Question: which of those four could actually catch this intruder, and what has to be true for each one to work? Name at least one that would catch nothing here, and tell me why. Those four — cameras, guards, motion sensors, employees — are exactly the four detection methods the College Board names, and by the end of class you'll know the strengths and the blind spots of each.

Section one. Four ways security controls detect a physical attack — cameras, security guards, motion sensors, and employees. As we go, watch for the condition each one needs to actually work.

[Slide 6]
Three equipment-based detection methods first. Cameras: they capture a visual record of an adversary's activity, and the feed should be both recorded AND monitored for maximum effect — recorded so you can investigate after an incident, monitored so someone can react during one. Security guards: they monitor an area and, crucially, can respond to suspicious activity the moment they detect it. A guard is the one method that detects and responds in the same person. Motion sensors: they alert security to movement in an area. Note what a sensor does NOT do — it doesn't tell you who is moving or whether it's a threat, only that something is moving. Write all three, and for each write the condition that makes it useful: a camera must be monitored or recorded, a guard must be present, a sensor must alert someone who responds.

[Slide 7]
The fourth detection method is the one students forget, because it isn't a gadget: employees that work in a physical space are often the first to notice the presence of an unauthorized person, and they can alert security. Think about why — employees know who belongs in their area, so an unfamiliar face stands out to them in a way no motion sensor could parse. They're already everywhere in the building during work hours, for free, with context that equipment doesn't have: the wrong uniform, an odd badge, someone in a place they shouldn't be. This is exactly why organizations train staff to report anything suspicious — 'see something, say something' is a detection control. The weakness is the flip side: it only works when people are present and paying attention. Overnight, in an empty building, your employees detect nothing — which is exactly where cameras and sensors earn their keep. Write it and its one weakness.

[Slide 8]
Step back for a second and place this lesson in the unit. Topic 2.3 was prevention: locks, access-control vestibules, managerial controls — everything designed to stop an adversary from getting in. But here's the security mindset the whole course is built on: assume prevention will sometimes fail. Topic 2.4 is the answer to 'what then?' These four methods are detective controls — their job isn't to stop the adversary at the door, it's to make sure that if the adversary does get in, they can't operate unnoticed. That's defense-in-depth: prevention as the first layer, detection as the next. On the exam, if a question describes noticing an attack rather than blocking it, you're in 2.4 territory. Write the distinction: 2.3 prevents, 2.4 detects.

[Slide 9]
Quick enrichment, because it sharpens a distinction the standard packs into one point. Says a camera feed should be recorded AND monitored for maximum effect — and those two words do different jobs. On the left, live detection: a monitored feed, a guard, a sensor, an alert employee — each can catch the attack while it's happening and let someone respond in time. On the right, the recorded record: camera footage reviewed after an incident, which can't stop what already happened but is invaluable for reconstructing the adversary's path and actions, for investigations, and for fixing the gap. Here's the trap: a camera that is only recorded and never monitored detects nothing in the moment — it's purely an after-the-fact tool. That's why the standard insists on both. Note which of the four methods are live, which produce a record, and which can do both.

After-incident investigation is the specific value of recorded footage. Know all six by name.
