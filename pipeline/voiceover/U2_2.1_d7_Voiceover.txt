[Slide 1]
Lesson 2.1. Yesterday you built a toolbox of security controls — physical, technical, managerial, and preventative, detective, corrective. Today, answers the obvious next question: why not just pick the one best control and be done? The College Board's answer is defense in depth, also called layered defense. The idea is that you use multiple types of controls together, for two specific reasons the exam tests. One: different threats need different controls, so layers let you address many threats at once. Two: any single control can be bypassed, so layers give you resilience — when an adversary defeats one, another still stands. By the end of class you'll be able to explain why defense in depth is effective using both reasons, in the College Board's own terms.

[Slide 2]
A medieval castle never trusted a single wall — moat, wall, gate, archers, inner keep, one behind the other. In three to five sentences: why didn't they just build one perfect wall? What do five different obstacles give you that one great obstacle can't? You just described defense in depth, and the two reasons you'll land on — different obstacles for different attackers, and a backup when one fails.

[Slide 6]
Start with what it is. Left column: a defense-in-depth strategy, also called a layered defense, uses multiple types of security controls to protect sensitive data and systems. The key phrase is 'multiple types' — you're combining the controls you classified yesterday, not stacking five copies of the same one. Right column: the layers can include human, physical, network, device, application, and data. Picture them from the outside in — the people, the building, the network connections, the individual devices, the applications, and at the center, the data itself. Each layer is a place to put a control.

[Slide 7]
Three cards, and the middle two are the answer to the exam's 'why' question. The first is the foundation: defense in depth uses multiple types of controls to protect data and systems. Now the two reasons it's effective. Coverage: it lets an organization address different types of threats, each with the control most suited to mitigate it — because no single control stops every kind of attack. A firewall won't stop a social engineer; a password policy won't stop a flood. Different threats, different controls. Resilience: when one control is bypassed by an adversary, another may still prevent access to the data or system, or at least limit the damage. That's the castle — get past the wall and the archers and keep are still there. Those are the two reasons that earn points.

[Slide 8]
Here's the trap. Layering sounds like it builds toward a perfect, unbreakable wall, so students claim defense in depth equals total security. But you already disproved that yesterday — absolute security is unattainable. What it actually promises is resilience: when one control is bypassed, another may still prevent access OR limit the damage. The realistic goal isn't 'never breached,' it's 'one failure doesn't become a catastrophe.' And it reminds you the layers must be different — they exist because different threats need different controls. Picture it: an attacker phishes a password and beats the human layer, but multi-factor authentication on the device blocks them, or monitoring catches the odd login and locks the account before data leaves. One layer fell; the game went on. Now stack five identical firewalls — beat one, beat all five. That's depth without diversity, and it misses entirely. Don't write 'unbreakable.' Write 'resilient.'

[Slide 9]
Let's make both reasons concrete, and connect them to the six layers. Left column is coverage: different threats, each met by the control best suited to it. Security training on the human layer stops social engineering; a firewall on the network layer stops external intrusion; locks and guards on the physical layer stop a break-in; encryption on the data layer protects stored data. No one control does all four jobs — that's why you need layers. Right column is resilience: the same attack, traced through the layers. An adversary phishes a password and beats the human layer — but multi-factor authentication on the device blocks them, monitoring on the network flags the strange login, and even if they break through, encryption on the data layer limits what they can steal. Here's the key skill: when the exam asks why defense in depth is effective, give BOTH reasons. Coverage and resilience. One reason is half an answer.

[Slide 10]
Five terms. Defense in depth and layered defense are the same thing — multiple types of controls protecting data and systems; know both names because the exam uses either. Then the two benefits that are the heart of the objective: coverage, addressing different threats each with the best-suited control; and resilience, where one bypassed control is backed by another that prevents access or limits damage. Finally, the security layers — human, physical, network, device, application, data. Be able to state coverage and resilience as the two reasons defense in depth works.

Question two: the password is phished and training fails — describe how another layer, say multi-factor authentication or encryption, still protects the data. That's resilience. Be precise.
