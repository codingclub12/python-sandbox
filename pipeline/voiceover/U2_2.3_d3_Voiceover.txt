Here's the model answer — the whole Topic 2.3 slice of Scenario 2A in one table. Read each row as a complete recommendation. Vehicles reaching the lobby glass: install bollards, with fencing and gates at the perimeter — that prevents, it deters and stops vehicle access. The propped-open side door letting anyone in: an access control vestibule or turnstile — prevents admitting unauthorized people, including by accident. No record of who enters restricted areas: card readers — that one detects, by logging which badge opened which door, and prevents, by denying unauthorized badges. The unlocked server cabinet: lock it, along with doors and computers — prevents devices being accessed or stolen. Free USB use on shared machines: disable the USB ports — prevents malware from external drives. And power loss threatening research systems: a UPS for critical devices plus a generator — that corrects, keeping devices alive through an outage. Notice every row names the control and prevent-detect-correct. Drop any one of those three and your answer is incomplete.

[Slide 8]
Let's run two of those gaps in full so you can see the reasoning the way the exam wants it. The propped-open side door, on the left. Adversary move: tailgate in behind staff, or just stroll through the propped door unchallenged. Control: an access control vestibule or turnstile, which prevents an authorized person from admitting an unauthorized one — on purpose or by accident. And pair it with security awareness training from Day 1, so staff stop propping the door in the first place — managerial and physical together. The unlocked server cabinet, on the right. Adversary move: reach the rack and pull or tamper with a drive. Control: a lock on the cabinet and the room door, which prevents devices being accessed or stolen. Add card readers on that door, for a record of who went in and when. That's the key skill — layering controls, even mixing managerial and physical, mitigates a vulnerability more completely than any single control.

[Slide 10]
Here's the Day 3 trap. It feels responsible to recommend everything and install it all now, and a giant list looks thorough. But Xtensr has a budget, and the standard says organizations prioritize mitigations by the severity of the risk and the cost of the fix. So your Scenario 2A deliverable isn't an unsorted wish list — it's a ranked plan. At Xtensr, an unlocked cabinet full of research data is severe and cheap to fix, just a lock, so it goes first. Bollards across the whole parking lot are expensive; if a vehicle ramming attack is low-likelihood here, they can wait. Recommend both, absolutely — but order them by severity and cost, and justify the order. That ordering, not the length of the list, is what scores.
