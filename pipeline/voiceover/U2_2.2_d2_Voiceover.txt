Two definitions the exam keeps strictly apart. On the left, threats: a threat is who or what could cause harm or disruption. The College Board names two kinds — human adversaries seeking to cause harm, and natural disasters. Don't skip the natural disasters; the standard says they can cause physical damage or destruction to computers and data and disrupt the digital services those computers provide. A flood is a threat just like a hacker is. On the right, vulnerabilities: a vulnerability is a weakness or flaw that could allow an asset to be compromised, and the standard lists four common compromises — unauthorized access to sensitive data or restricted spaces, disruption of services, theft or destruction of resources, and unauthorized modification of data. Threat is the who-or-what; vulnerability is the weakness; the four bullets are what goes wrong.

[Slide 8]
Three categories of harm physical access enables. First, disrupt power: cut the power and the device and every service it provides go dark — that's a disruption. The standard gives the methods verbatim: damaging fuses or breakers in an electrical box, unplugging or cutting wiring, or damaging power distribution systems like substations and transformers. Second, steal or copy information: get into an area with sensitive information and you can take it or copy it — that's loss of confidentiality. Third, attack the device through its ports: plug in a keylogger or an external drive carrying malware to collect data from a user or even gain control of the device, and with direct access you can physically destroy the device, making it, its data, and its services unavailable — that's destruction. Write all three, and tag each with which outcome word it produces: disruption, loss, destruction.

Six terms for today. [Slide 10]
Here's the misconception the College Board builds into. The word 'threat' sounds like a person — a thief, a hacker — and after a whole day of human attacks yesterday, it's natural to assume threats are always people. But read: threats include human adversaries AND natural disasters. A flood, a fire, a storm — each can physically damage or destroy computers and data and disrupt the services they provide. Same outcomes as a human attacker: loss, damage, disruption, destruction. Picture a data center in a flood plain: no attacker, but one storm destroys the servers, corrupts the backups, and takes services down for days. The vulnerability there is poor siting, and the threat exploiting it is weather. That's why real physical-security plans include fire suppression and flood control, not just locks. On the exam, if a scenario describes weather wrecking equipment, that's still a threat — don't write it off.
