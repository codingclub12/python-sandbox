[Slide 6]
Here is why defenders reach for AI, in two columns. On the left, the flood: a real network produces millions of events a day — logins, file accesses, connections — and the overwhelming majority are perfectly normal. But some small number are an adversary at work, and the College Board states it plainly: humans cannot carefully examine all those events to find the malicious activity. The math simply doesn't work. On the right, the triage: AI-powered tools can be trained to quickly analyze those events and sort the likely-malicious from the harmless. That's the ‘more accurate’ half of today's objective — AI shrinks millions of events down to the handful a human should actually look at. [Slide 7]
Section four. Same objective. Triage finds the suspicious event — now what? AI alerts a human or takes action, and that speed is what lets a team stop the damage.

[Slide 8]
Three capabilities, in the order they actually happen. First, sort: the AI, trained on what normal and malicious traffic look like, turns millions of events into a short list of likely-malicious ones. Second, alert or act: the tool is programmed either to alert human cybersecurity personnel, or to take a specific corrective action chosen for the type of activity — isolating a device, blocking a connection. Note the ‘or’ — it can hand off to a human, or it can act, depending on how it's set up. Third, intervene quickly: because all of this happens in seconds rather than days, threat-detection and response teams can catch the attack and step in fast enough to prevent loss, harm, damage, and destruction. That speed is the ‘faster’ half of the objective. Write all three.

[Slide 9]
This is the Day 2 version of yesterday's misconception, and it's just as tempting. The standard says the AI can take corrective action on its own, so students conclude the analysts are obsolete — the machine watches and acts, done. But read all four ideas together. AI exists here to beat scale and speed: it triages the millions of events no human could read, and it can react in milliseconds. What it does NOT do is replace judgment. The standard has it alert human personnel, and names human response teams as the ones who intervene. Picture it: AI can auto-isolate a device behaving like ransomware in the blink of an eye — but a person decides if it was truly an attack, how far it reached, what to restore, whether to call it an incident. Give the tool all the judgment and one false positive could take a hospital offline; take the tool away and the real attack vanishes into four million events. That is why the College Board keeps both — the same human-in-the-loop you met yesterday, now on response.

[Slide 10]
Let's carry Scenario 1E forward and connect the whole unit. Yesterday, before launch, AI helped secure the code — that was yesterday. Now the app is live, and here's why today matters. On the left: an adversary probes those input fields for anything the team missed, and remember from Lesson 1.4 that AI-augmented attacks come faster and at greater scale. Those probe attempts are just a few events buried in millions; no human staring at a dashboard catches them in time. On the right: trained AI sorts the probes out of the harmless traffic; it alerts the security team and can automatically block the source; and the response team intervenes quickly, before any data is read or altered. Here's the key skill: AI secured the app before launch, and defends it after — AI on defense across the entire life of the application. And it's the answer to Lesson 1.4: when attacks move at AI speed, only AI-assisted defense can move fast enough to meet them.

[Slide 11]
Five terms for today. The two the exam leans on are AI event triage — sorting malicious from harmless — and the threat detection and response team, the humans who intervene. A digital event is just any recorded activity on the network, and there are millions a day. Automated alert or corrective action is the step — and notice it's ‘alert OR act.’ And know the term false positive: a harmless event flagged as malicious, which is exactly why a human stays in the loop before anything drastic is done automatically.
