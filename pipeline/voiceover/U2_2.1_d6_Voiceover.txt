Lesson 2.1. Yesterday, mitigation meant 'implement security controls to reduce a risk.' Today, we open the toolbox and learn what those controls actually are and how the College Board classifies them. There are two classification systems, and the exam expects you to use both. First, by type: physical, technical, and managerial — where the control lives. Second, by function: preventive, detective, and corrective — what the control does about an attack. And underneath all of it, every control serves at least one of three principles: confidentiality, integrity, or availability. By the end of class you'll be able to take any control — a lock, a firewall, a password policy — and classify it both ways.

Picture how a bank guards its money: a locked vault and a guard out front, an alarm and cameras recording everyone, and a rule that no one employee can open the vault alone. In three to five sentences, sort those into groups that feel alike and say what makes each group different. You're about to discover you naturally grouped them two ways — by where they live and by what they do — which is exactly how the College Board classifies controls.

[Slide 5]
Section six of the lesson. First what controls protect, then the two ways to classify them. As we go, keep asking two questions of every control: where does it live, and what does it do?

[Slide 6]
Before we classify controls, know what they're for. The standard says every security control addresses at least one of three principles. Left column: confidentiality ensures only authorized individuals, systems, or processes can access data; integrity ensures data are accurate and trustworthy; availability ensures data and services are accessible to authorized people when needed. Right column is the exam's favorite move — what happens when each is missing. No confidentiality and you're vulnerable to data theft or destruction. No integrity and you're vulnerable to data manipulation. No availability and you get unexpected downtime. Memorize the triad and memorize each failure mode — the College Board pairs them constantly.

[Slide 7]
First classification, by type — where the control lives. All three are core. Physical controls provide security in the physical space: locks, fences, cameras, bollards, security guards — anything protecting the building and the hardware. Technical controls provide security in the digital space: firewalls, anti-malware software, encryption — technology defending data and systems. Managerial controls are the rules: policies, guidelines, and procedures that specify what security should be in place — password policies, regular access reviews, incident response plans. A quick note on the objective wording: you'll sometimes see 'operational' listed as a fourth type, but the College Board names three — physical, technical, managerial — so those three are what you cite.

[Slide 8]
Second classification, by function — what the control does. Think of an attack's timeline. Before: preventative controls address potential vulnerabilities to stop an adversary from attacking — locks and encryption. During: detective controls help identify attacks when they occur — intrusion detection systems, cameras, SIEM systems. After: corrective controls fix problems and restore systems to an operational state — vulnerability patching, repairing a broken card reader, intrusion prevention systems. Before, during, after — that's the cleanest way to keep them straight. And notice a control can be both: a camera is a physical control by type and a detective control by function.

[Slide 9]
Here's the misconception that costs points. Because the categories are taught as one list, students treat them as exclusive slots and give a control one label. But there are two independent classification systems. One is by type — where the control lives: physical, technical, managerial. The other is by function — what it does: preventative, detective, corrective. Every control gets a label on each axis. A camera is physical by type and detective by function. Encryption is technical and preventative. A lock is physical and preventative. Take a SIEM: technical by type, detective by function — answer only one and you've answered half. The exam routinely asks you to classify one control both ways in a single prompt, so train the habit: where does it live, AND what does it do? Both, every time.

[Slide 10]
Seven terms, the full classification vocabulary. Start with the C-I-A triad, the principles every control serves. Then the three types: physical lives in physical space, technical in digital space, managerial sets the rules. Then the three functions: preventative stops the attack, detective spots it, corrective fixes the damage. For each function memorize its example controls — locks and encryption prevent, IDS and cameras and SIEM detect, patching and IPS correct. Remember every control takes one type label and one function label.

[Slide 11]
Question one: laptop encryption — classify it both ways. By type it's technical; by function it's preventative; and the principle it most protects is confidentiality. Both labels and the principle. Question two: a guard, an IDS, and a patching process — classify each by function. The guard prevents, the IDS detects, the patching corrects — and explain how that covers before, during, and after an attack. Be precise.
