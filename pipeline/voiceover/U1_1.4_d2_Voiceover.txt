[Slide 5]
Section three of the lesson, and today's whole focus. Four defenses. The standard is specific about all four — know each one, know which attack it counters, and know that each one works without you having to detect the fake.

[Slide 6]
Three defenses for identity and data. First, the shared secret word — your bell ringer. It defeats a perfect voice clone because the clone copies your sound, not your secrets; it was never told the word. Second, MFA. This is the answer when the attack targets voice-based authentication on a system: even a cloned voice that beats the voiceprint still hits a wall at the one-time code. Third, don't feed personal or sensitive data into AI tools. Remember from yesterday — models can leak what was put into them — so the defense is to not put sensitive things in. Write all three.

[Slide 7]
The fourth defense: carefully evaluate AI output. On the left is the risk, and it has two sources. One is the poisoning attack from yesterday — where an adversary plants false information specifically so the model trains on it and repeats it. The other is simpler: AI tools are sometimes just wrong, no attacker required. Either way, a confident tone is not proof of truth. On the right is the defense: verify information from AI tools against reputable, stable, non-AI-based sources before you act, especially for anything high-stakes. Treat AI output as a lead to check, not a verdict to trust. This is the one defense that protects the integrity of what you learn, rather than your identity or your data.

[Slide 8]
This is the misconception that trips up strong students, because it sounds sophisticated. The instinct is: an AI attack is high-tech, so my defense should be high-tech — a detector app, an AI that catches the other AI. But think about what you proved yesterday: detection is exactly what AI takes away, and detectors are in an arms race they can lose as clones improve. Now look at the defenses. A secret word is almost embarrassingly low-tech, and it wins one hundred percent of the time, because the attacker cannot produce a word they were never given. That is the deep lesson of this whole topic: the College Board's defenses don't try to out-detect the AI. They make detection unnecessary — a secret, a second factor, data you never shared, a source you check independently. Simpler, and stronger.

[Slide 10]
Here is Scenario 1D solved. On the left, the chain you traced yesterday: recon scrapes the voice and finds the relative; cloning turns those samples into the avatar; the call delivers the perfect-sounding emergency. The relative cannot detect the fake — that's the trap. On the right, the defenses, matched to the moment. The decisive one is: the relative asks for the secret word and the clone goes silent, because it was never told. You can also shrink the recon surface by keeping less public, use MFA if any account voice-authentication is in play, and adopt the verify-second-channel mindset before wiring money. But circle the key skill: the winning move works even though no one ever detected the fake. That is the entire lesson in one sentence.

If it hands you account access via voice authentication, the answer is MFA. Match the defense to the attack.
